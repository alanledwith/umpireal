The Policy Gap

What Exists.
What Doesn't.
What Must.

Environmental transport policy operates on a fundamental assumption: that the engine is the problem. From Euro 1 in 1992 through to the proposed Euro 7, every regulation has targeted combustion. This produced real, meaningful gains. But it left the deeper source of inefficiency untouched — the infrastructure that forces engines to waste the energy they generate.

Engine Standard ✓ Exists
WHAT IT MEASURES
Pollutant emissions per kilometre under test conditions
WHAT IT REGULATES
Combustion quality, exhaust aftertreatment, fuel injection
COMPLIANCE MECHANISM
Type approval. Cannot sell without it.
DECADES IN OPERATION
30+ years. Euro 1 → Euro 7.
RESULT
90% reduction in NOx and PM. Cleaner combustion achieved.
Infrastructure Standard ✗ Missing
WHAT IT WOULD MEASURE
Energy preserved as useful motion per network km
WHAT IT WOULD REGULATE
Junction design, crossing geometry, flow interruption rate
COMPLIANCE MECHANISM
Not yet defined. Does not yet exist.
DECADES IN OPERATION
Zero. Never attempted.
POTENTIAL RESULT
30–50% system efficiency gain. Fuel savings. CO₂ reduction. Lives saved.
THE PROPOSED STANDARD
An Infrastructure Efficiency Rating
A network-level metric that assesses what proportion of energy introduced into a road system is preserved as useful forward motion across all vehicle types. Networks that force frequent stops — through signalised junctions, pedestrian crossings, and managed chokepoints — would perform poorly. Networks designed to preserve momentum continuously would perform well. The metric is physics-based, powertrain-agnostic, and measurable today with existing sensor technology.

If Europe can set a standard for how cleanly fuel burns, it can set a standard for how often we force vehicles to waste it.

A Call To Policy Action

Five Things
A Government
Can Do Now

Umpireal is not asking governments to fund a technology before it is proven. We are asking policymakers to recognise a structural gap in the regulatory framework — and to begin closing it. These five actions require no capital commitment. They require only the recognition that an infrastructure efficiency gap exists.

1
Commission an Infrastructure Efficiency Baseline
Fund a study that measures, for the first time, the percentage of vehicle energy wasted through forced stops across the national road network. This is the data that no government currently holds — and without it, meaningful progress is impossible.
2
Establish a Definition of Momentum-Preserving Infrastructure
Direct transport agencies to develop a technical definition of what constitutes a momentum-preserving network design. This creates the regulatory foundation on which a future standard can be built — and which innovation can be assessed against.
3
Create a Pilot Designation for Momentum Infrastructure Projects
Allow cities and local authorities to designate pilot corridors where momentum-preserving infrastructure can be deployed and measured. This generates the real-world data necessary to underpin a future standard — at no risk to national policy commitments.
4
Include Infrastructure Efficiency in Climate Transport Targets
National Renewable Energy Plans and Climate Action Plans currently focus almost entirely on vehicle electrification and fuel switching. Adding a measurable infrastructure efficiency component would unlock a 30–50% gain that no vehicle technology can deliver on its own.
5
Engage With Umpireal
We have built the technical case. We have the physics. We have the IP. We have the business model. What we are seeking from government is the regulatory environment in which momentum-preserving infrastructure can be tested, scaled, and standardised. Let's build that together.
