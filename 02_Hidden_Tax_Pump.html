First Tax — Compounding Fuel Taxation

How the State
Taxes the Tax

In Ireland and across most European jurisdictions, motorists do not pay a flat fuel tax. They pay excise duty, then carbon tax, then NORA and energy levies — and then VAT is applied on top of all of those taxes. The State literally taxes its own taxes. Every time pump prices rise, VAT revenue rises automatically, creating a treasury windfall while motorists carry the cost.

The forecourt retailer — often blamed for high prices — retains only a few cents per litre to cover wages, electricity, rent, maintenance and insurance. The apparent price rise is not retail profit. It is compounding taxation.

In many energy markets, renewable electricity prices are indexed to fossil-fuel prices — meaning that when gas or oil prices surge, renewable generators benefit from the same increase despite having stable production costs. High energy prices are therefore partly a structural feature, not a temporary anomaly.

"The real issue is not the price of fuel. It is the multiple ways energy is quietly drained from the economy before it ever performs useful work."

The Conclusion

Two Taxes.
One Solution.

€1.26
Of every €2.10 spent at the pump, €1.26 goes directly to the State. The driver, the refinery, the tanker, the forecourt and everyone else in the supply chain share the remaining 84 cents. And that's before the road destroys the energy you just purchased.

Tax at the pump compounds automatically with every price rise. But the second tax — the infrastructure tax — is invisible, uncollected and unlimited. Every junction, every signalised crossing, every speed bump forces vehicles to discard momentum they have already paid for and buy it back again at 12–20% engine efficiency.

Together, these financial and physical losses form a hidden energy penalty that inflates costs, erodes productivity and undermines the efficiency of modern cities — regardless of whether energy is fossil-based or renewable.

Improving fuel efficiency at the engine is useful. But no matter how efficient an engine becomes, if the road network is designed to repeatedly strip momentum, a significant portion of that efficiency is dissipated as waste heat before it performs useful work.
