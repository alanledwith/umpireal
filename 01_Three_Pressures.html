The Problem Statement

Three Crises.
One Root Cause.

Debates about the cost of living, climate change and urban dysfunction often become politically polarised. Yet regardless of where one stands in any of those debates, three realities are becoming increasingly difficult to ignore. Across towns and cities worldwide, daily life is being shaped by three converging pressures — and they are accelerating.

⚡
Energy Affordability
Households, businesses and public services face escalating energy costs that feel impossible to control — even when global markets temporarily stabilise.
🏗
Housing Shortages
Restricted opportunity, reduced mobility and growing inequality flow from the same infrastructure systems that make urban land so expensive to build on.
🚦
Traffic Congestion
Time wasted, productivity eroded, and enormous quantities of fuel consumed — without producing a single unit of economic value. Stop-go infrastructure is a machine for destroying wealth.

"These challenges are often discussed as separate policy problems. In reality, they are deeply connected — reinforced by the same underlying systems."

The Hidden Connection

Why Stop-Go
Makes Everything
Worse

To understand why energy feels increasingly unaffordable, we must look beyond global oil markets and headline fuel prices. And to understand congestion, we must look beyond traffic volumes alone. In both cases, the deeper issue lies in how energy is taxed, how it is wasted, and how urban infrastructure is designed to destroy it.

This is where the real cost begins to emerge. Not in the oil field, not in the refinery, not even at the pump — but in the repeated, deliberate stripping of momentum that cities embed into their own road networks, junction by junction, crossing by crossing.

The real question is not how efficiently we can recover energy after it has been destroyed. It is why we choose to destroy it so often in the first place.

About This Series

The Momentum Papers

This is Publication 01 of 12 in a series examining urban infrastructure failure — and the physical, financial and civic case for a fundamentally different approach. Each publication isolates one dimension of the problem or the solution. Together, they form a complete case for why momentum-preserving infrastructure is the next great challenge in urban design.

Subsequent publications examine the hidden energy taxes at the pump, the physics of stop-start driving, why EVs don't solve this, the insurance cascade triggered by junction accidents, and the Umpireal solution itself.
