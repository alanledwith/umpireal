Three Decades of Engine Policy

The Standards
That Cleaned
The Engine

Beginning in 1992, Europe embarked on one of the most ambitious regulatory campaigns in industrial history. The Euro emissions standards — progressing from Euro 1 through to the current Euro 6 — created a global template. The United States followed with EPA Tier standards. India adopted Bharat Stage. China developed China VI. All shared the same premise: regulate the engine as the primary source of environmental harm.

The results were real. Nitrogen oxides, particulate matter and harmful pollutants have been reduced by more than ninety percent since the early 1990s. This should not be understated. But it tells only half the story.

1992 — Euro 1
First regulated limits on CO, HC, NOx, PM

Diesel particulate filters become mandatory
Added €1,000–€2,000 per vehicle. DPF failures emerging in stop-start urban use. Road infrastructure: unchanged.
VI
2015 — Euro 6
SCR systems, AdBlue infrastructure, advanced sensors
Added €1,500–€3,000+ per passenger vehicle. €5,000–€10,000 per HGV. Dieselgate exposed compliance workarounds. Road infrastructure: unchanged.
7
2026+ — Euro 7 (Proposed)
Brake dust, tyre particulates, cold-start emissions
Targeting the residual 10%. Manufacturers warn of significant cost. Road infrastructure: still unchanged.

Thirty years of regulation targeted the engine. Not once did policy ask how many times the road forces the engine to restart from zero.

The Flaw In The Framework

Cleaning
Combustion
Is Not The
Same As
Reducing It

There is a distinction that transport policy has never properly confronted. Emissions standards were designed to clean combustion — to reduce the harmful byproducts of burning fuel. They were never designed to reduce the amount of fuel burned. And that distinction is now the defining limit of three decades of regulatory effort.

The mass of CO₂ produced during combustion is directly proportional to the carbon content of the fuel. A litre of fuel will produce broadly the same quantity of CO₂ regardless of how advanced the engine is. Cleaner combustion does not mean less combustion.

THE MISSING STANDARD
Europe has a rigorous, evolving standard for engines. It has no equivalent standard for roads.
A meaningful infrastructure efficiency standard would assess how much energy introduced into a road network is preserved as useful forward motion — and how much is lost as waste heat through forced deceleration and re-acceleration. No such metric exists. No such target has ever been set. This is the structural gap that Umpireal is designed to fill.
